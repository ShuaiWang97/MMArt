\begin{abstract}
Interpreting a painting is not a single act of perception: an expert viewer simultaneously identifies what is depicted, analyzes how it is painted, feels its emotional register, and situates it within art history. Yet existing multimodal art datasets train systems to produce only one of these at a time, leaving the richer interpretive space largely uncovered. We introduce \textbf{PolyArt}, a large-scale dataset of approximately 70{,}000 paintings from WikiArt, each annotated with four independently generated perspectives: \emph{Narrative and Scene Interpretation}, \emph{Formal Visual Analysis}, \emph{Emotional Response}, and \emph{Historical and Contextual Analysis}. A fifth \emph{unified caption} harmonizes all four into a single coherent description. Perspectives are generated by specialized vision-language models chosen for their fit to each perspective type---Qwen2.5-VL-72B for narrative and historical analysis, GalleryGPT for formal analysis, and Qwen3-VL-8B with ArtEmis context for emotional response---ensuring each perspective is grounded in model capabilities aligned with the underlying interpretive task.

Beyond its value as a descriptive resource, PolyArt supports two benchmark contributions. First, a \emph{perspective complementarity} experiment tests whether the four perspectives encode genuinely distinct information: given only the text of one or more perspectives, can a generative model reconstruct the original painting? Fidelity is measured across four dimensions---style, composition, iconographic content, and emotional tone---yielding empirical evidence that each perspective recovers different aspects of the image. Second, a \emph{reasoning mode alignment} experiment evaluates whether agentic systems prompted with perspective-appropriate reasoning strategies (formal, causal, semiotic, phenomenological, analogical, counterfactual) outperform those given generic instructions on perspective-specific generation tasks. Together, PolyArt establishes a structured benchmark for multi-perspective art understanding, with implications for vision-language model evaluation, multimodal retrieval, and the study of interpretive diversity in AI systems.
\end{abstract}
